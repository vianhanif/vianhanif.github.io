Every post on this blog has a little badge at the bottom that says "CC BY 4.0." I put it there deliberately, and I want to explain what it means and why I chose it.

## What It Is

[CC BY 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/) (Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International) is a permissive open license for creative work. It means you can take anything I write here, copy it, remix it, build on it, sell it, translate it, turn it into a video, stick it in a book — do whatever you want — as long as you credit me as the original author.

That's basically it. One rule: attribution.

## Why I Picked This License

I thought about this a lot before putting content online. A few options were on the table:

- **All rights reserved.** Standard copyright. Nobody touches anything without asking. Feels restrictive for what I'm trying to do.
- **CC BY-NC.** People can share, but not for commercial use. The problem: "commercial use" is vague. Does a company sharing my post internally count? What if a newsletter with ads quotes me? I'd rather not play whack-a-mole with edge cases.
- **CC BY-SA.** Share-alike means derivative works must use the same license. That's fine for code, but for writing? I want people to be able to quote me in a book without the whole book needing to be CC-licensed.
- **CC BY 4.0.** One simple rule. Credit me. Go nuts.

I landed on CC BY because my goal is *reach*, not control. I write to share ideas, not to gatekeep them. If someone finds my post useful enough to republish or adapt, I want that person to hit zero legal friction. Attribution is my only ask — it ensures the work stays connected to me even when it spreads.

## A Few Important Details

CC BY 4.0 doesn't require share-alike. If you adapt my post and publish it, you can license your version however you want. You just have to note that the original was mine and link back to it.

Commercial use is allowed. You can include my writing in a paid product or sell derivative works. I want this to be true because I don't want to be the arbiter of who "deserves" to use my content. Let the market figure it out.

No DRM or technical restrictions. If you share my work, you can't lock it behind a paywall or a login gate in a way that prevents others from exercising the same rights I gave you.

## One Caveat

The license covers *my writing*. Code snippets in posts may be under a different license (usually MIT or Apache 2.0). If you're copying a code block, check the repo.

Everything else? Take it. Share it. Adapt it. Just link back.

That's the deal.
