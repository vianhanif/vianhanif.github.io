# Medium Prep

## Content to Copy

When you start a blog using a popular Jekyll theme, you get a lot of things for free: responsive layout, dark mode, fast load times, and a little Creative Commons badge in the footer that you didn't ask for.

That was my experience with the Chirpy theme. It ships with CC BY 4.0 enabled by default in its locale data. For weeks I didn't think about it — it was just open-source boilerplate, the kind of thing everyone in the tech blogging community has.

But one day I actually clicked the link. And I realized I had no idea what I'd signed up for.

The Creative Commons family is bigger than most people realize: BY (attribution only), BY-SA (share-alike), BY-NC (non-commercial), BY-ND (no derivatives). Each one makes a different promise about how others can use your work. The 4.0 version, updated in 2013, includes international language so it holds up across legal jurisdictions — but the core choice is still yours.

I realized that leaving the default was itself a decision — just an unexamined one. A blog's license is like its terms of service. If it's vague, readers and reusers have to guess. That ambiguity creates friction for the people I actually want to share my work: hobbyists, students, educators, other writers.

I landed on CC BY-NC 4.0. It keeps what I want — sharing, adapting, quoting, remixing — while adding one boundary: non-commercial. If you're a blogger quoting a paragraph, go ahead. If you're building a paid course around my writing, let's talk. That "let's talk" creates a conversation instead of a vacuum.

There's also the Medium angle. I cross-post some stories there, and I might enable the partner program. The license doesn't stop me from doing that — as the copyright holder, I retain the right to monetize my own work. What it does prevent is someone else scraping my writing and republishing it behind their own paywall. That distinction matters.

Is NC fuzzy? Absolutely. Company newsletters, Patreon creators, ad-supported blogs — all gray. But a fuzzy line is better than no line. It signals intent and forces a conversation.
